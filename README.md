# study-of-lemur-poop

A playful-but-professional repository documenting an observational study of lemur scat (poop, droppings, feces) for educational and non-invasive biological field-study purposes.

---

## Overview

This repository collects notes, images, and field observations about lemur fecal morphology and ecological clues gleaned from non-invasive scat surveys. The goal is to provide an accessible reference for students, naturalists, and researchers interested in using fecal observations as a complementary tool in behavioral ecology, diet inference, and habitat assessment—without promoting harmful or invasive practices.

Key aims:
- Standardize descriptive terminology for field notes.
- Provide photo examples to aid recognition and training.
- Offer ethical and safety guidance for responsible fieldwork.
- Seed ideas for further, properly permitted scientific studies.

---

## Why it matters

Scat is more than a smelly surprise on the trail—it's an ecological data packet. Careful, non-invasive observation of lemur feces can inform:
- Diet composition and seasonal shifts (fruit, leaves, nectar, insects).
- Foraging behavior and habitat use.
- Seed dispersal patterns and plant–lemur interactions.
- Presence/absence and relative activity of elusive species.
- Baseline data that can support conservation and management decisions.

When used responsibly, scat observations are a low-impact way to gather meaningful natural-history information.

## Morphology notes

When describing lemur feces in the field, consider standardized morphology features:

- Shape
  - Pelleted (small, discrete pellets; common in some folivores/lemur species)
  - Clumped or tubular (larger-bodied species or frugivorous meals)
- Size
  - Note approximate length/diameter or compare to common objects (e.g., "about the size of a coin")
- Consistency
  - Dry/crumbling, firm, soft, or mucous-coated
- Color
  - Dark (highly processed plant material), greenish (fresh vegetation), reddish/bright (recent fruit/berry ingestion)
- Visible contents
  - Seeds, seed coats, leaf fragments, insect exoskeletons, hair
- Odor (describe qualitatively; avoid detailed olfactory protocols)

Use consistent descriptors and, when possible, take a scale photo to accompany the note.

## Habitat / diet clues

Scat can carry immediate clues about a lemur's recent diet and the habitat it used:

- High seed content and large, smooth seeds → frugivory; suggests availability of fruiting trees nearby.
- High fiber and leaf fragments → folivory; indicates use of foliage-rich strata.
- Insect fragments, small chitinous parts → insectivory or supplemental arthropod feeding.
- Frequent small, hard pellets → may indicate species with more abrasive diets or gut morphologies that produce pelletized feces.
- Seeds that are intact vs. pitted → whether seeds are being dispersed intact (beneficial to plants) or crushed (predation on seeds).
- Location context: scat found on trail vs. canopy fall vs. latrine tree can indicate territorial behavior, movement corridors, or communal sites.

Caution: diet inferences from scat are indicative, not definitive. For rigorous diet composition or nutritional analysis, partner with permitted labs and specialists.

## Ethics / Safety

Safety and ethics are paramount. Follow these principles:

- Permits & approvals: Obtain all required research permits, park permissions, and institutional approvals before fieldwork.
- Non-invasive first: Prioritize observation and photography. Avoid disturbing animals or their habitat to collect samples.
- Minimize disturbance: Keep a respectful distance, limit noise and group size, and avoid trampling vegetation.
- Personal safety & PPE: Use gloves when handling any material, wash hands thoroughly, and have basic first-aid supplies on hand.
- Biohazard caution: Scat can harbor pathogens. Do NOT attempt culture or laboratory work without proper biosafety facilities and trained personnel.
- Proper disposal: If disposing of materials or gloves, follow local guidelines for biological waste.
- Data stewardship: Treat location data sensitively—precise coordinates for rare/endangered species should be protected to prevent poaching or disturbance.
- Attribution & collaboration: Share findings openly when appropriate and acknowledge local guides, communities, and authorities.

If in doubt, consult wildlife veterinarians, park managers, or institutional biosafety officers.

## Future work

Potential directions to expand this resource:

- Standardized field datasheets and a mobile-friendly data-entry form.
- Training modules for students and citizen scientists on non-invasive scat observation.
- Geospatial visualizations of scat observations (with sensitive data handling).
- Collaboration with labs for DNA metabarcoding, stable isotope work, or parasite surveys—conducted only under appropriate permits and biosafety conditions.
- Comparative morphology guide by species and region (with voucher-backed specimens where ethically sourced).

Contributions, issues, and pull requests are welcome—particularly for improving terminology, image metadata, and educational materials.
